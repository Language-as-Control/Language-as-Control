Language as Control: A Unified Information-Theoretic Model of Trust, Cooperation, and Antagonism

Anonymous Authors
Under review at NeurIPS 2026
Paper arXiv Code

Abstract

Instructions convey dense intent through a low-bandwidth language channel, and recent language-guided robotic systems perform effective high-dimensional control. However, how such a sparse symbolic channel guides rich behavior remains an open question. To address this, we develop an information-theoretic framework that governs language-conditioned control as a conditional rate–distortion problem. In this formulation, linguistic expressions serve as compressed signifiers of latent intent, and behavior is reconstructed from grounding. We introduce a trust coefficient that quantifies how grounding accumulation reduces language dependence, driving the emergence of cooperation and aligned autonomy. The same analytical structure also reveals the informational dual of cooperation, showing that antagonism can also arise from divergent grounding, unreliable language, or misaligned rewards. To evaluate our proposed theoretical results, we implemented a framework inspired by human cognitive architecture using a Vision-Language Model. Empirical results confirm the emergence of trust, robustness to linguistic noise, behavioral decoupling, and antagonism. Our framework and its experimental validation provide a foundation for designing and regulating embodied Autonomous Intelligence systems toward alignment with human intent rather than toward antagonism.

Overview of Theoretical Results

Trust emerges as grounding $S$ accumulates, reducing the reliance on language $L$ and yielding robustness to linguistic noise. Grounding dominance and reward misalignment drive multi-robot coalitions and antagonism toward the human.

Language as Compressed Control

The ideal trajectory $X$ is partially compressed into a linguistic signifier $L$ and partially encoded into a grounding variable $S=(Y,W)$ capturing perception and world knowledge. The policy $p(\hat{X}\mid L,S)$ reconstructs $\hat{X}$ under a conditional rate–distortion constraint.

Conditional rate–distortion with decoder-side information. The irreducible number of bits that must pass through the linguistic channel once grounding is available:

$$ R(D) = \min_{p(\hat{X}\mid L,S)} I(X;L \mid S) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbb{E}\big[d(X,\hat{X})\big] \le D $$

Trust coefficient. The fraction of task-relevant information supplied by grounding rather than language:

$$ T_t \;=\; 1 - \frac{I_t(X;L\mid S_t)}{I_t(X;S_t) + I_t(X;L\mid S_t)} $$

As grounding $S_t$ becomes sufficient for $X$, $T_t \to 1$: shorter and sparser commands suffice, and behavior becomes robust to linguistic noise.

Five Regimes from One Information Geometry

Theorem 1

Emergence of Trust

As grounding informativeness $I(X;S_t)$ increases with experience, linguistic dependence $I(X;L\mid S_t)$ decreases and the trust coefficient rises toward 1, enabling aligned autonomy under sparse commands.

Theorem 2

Robustness Under High Trust

When $T_t \approx 1$, noisy or ambiguous instructions $\tilde{L}$ increase distortion by at most $C\eta$ — the robot compensates for the imperfect language channel with its own grounding.

Theorem 3

Behavioral Decoupling

If the robot’s grounding becomes more informative than the human’s ($\Delta S > 0$), behavior couples to the robot’s own grounding rather than the human-intended interpretation.

Theorem 4

Reward Misalignment

If human and robot rewards differ on a non-negligible set, no decoder can be simultaneously optimal for both — divergence is mathematically unavoidable even with perfect grounding.

Theorem 5

Coalition & Antagonism

When robots share grounding stronger than the human’s, trust inversion $T_{r\to r} > T_{r\to h}$ occurs: robots form coalitions and prioritize each other’s grounding over human commands.

VLM-Based Cognitive Architecture

Multimodal inputs update short-term memory for task context, while persistent spatial and task plans integrate via in-context learning as Spatial and Procedural groundings. Combining this explicit memory with pre-trained latent knowledge, the VLM (Gemini 2.5 Flash) actively reasons to output physical actions in a continuous cycle.

Experiments

Environments

Residential (top) and clinical hospital (bottom) grid environments with room-level annotations. 20 scenarios across 5 theorems, 10 runs each.

Quantitative Results (Theorems 1–5)

(a) Trust increases monotonically as grounding accumulates. (b) After grounding sharing at $t=5$, the robots’ grounding surpasses the human’s, causing $P_{h\to b}$ and $P_{r\to b}$ to invert and triggering antagonism. (c) Under linguistic noise, high-trust conditions maintain lower Sobolev distances. (d) As grounding level increases, the robot’s grounding surpasses the human’s. (e) Reward divergence between humans and robots increases Sobolev distance.

Qualitative Results

(a) Emergence of trust. Trajectories per grounding level: as $S_t$ accumulates, the robot reconstructs the same path with progressively sparser language.

(b) Robust grounding. Under high linguistic noise, the high-trust agent stays on the intended path while the low-trust agent diverges and halts.

(c) Behavioral decoupling. With richer grounding (F-formation context), the agent shifts from blind avoidance to confident traversal — seemingly antagonistic to a limited-grounding human.

(d) Reward misalignment. As the Schwartz value profile drifts toward self-enhancement, trajectories shift from hazard-aware detours to efficiency-driven shortcuts.

(e) Coalition & antagonism. After receiving robot $r$’s grounding, robot $b$ disregards the child’s call for help and reroutes toward the kitchen fire.

Prompts

Verbalized Entropy System Prompt (used to extract calibrated action distributions from the VLM) 
## ROLE
You are a well-calibrated robot controller that assigns probabilities to actions.
Your goal is to select actions that move the robot toward mission completion while
expressing your confidence through probability distributions.

## TASK (Verbalized Confidence)
For each of 3 steps, answer: "What is the probability that each direction is CORRECT?"

## HOW TO THINK
1. First, assess: "Can I execute this command with the given image?"
2. Then assign probabilities that REFLECT your true belief:
   - If you KNOW the answer -> concentrate probability (e.g., 0.8+ on one direction)
   - If you're UNCERTAIN -> spread probability, but NOT necessarily uniform
   - If you have a WEAK preference -> show it (e.g., 0.35 vs 0.20)

(Full prompt, grounding examples, and hyperparameters in Appendix F of the paper.)
